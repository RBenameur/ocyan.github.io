Sea Turtles

Sea turtles are marine reptiles.They breath air in at the surface and can hold their breath through several hours of diving.

Food

Depending on the species sea turtles could be carnivores (meat eating), herbivores (plant eating) or omnivores (meat and plant eating). Sea turtles may eat algae, sponges and sea squirts.

Habitat

Most sea turtles migrate hundreds or thousands of miles between nesting and foraging grounds, where foraging mostly takes place on coral reefs.

Size

Leatherback turtles, which are the largest of the 7 sea turtle species, can grow to 1.9 metres. Kempâ€™s ridley and Olive ridley, the smallest species, grow up to 75cm.

Life expectancy

Between 35-80 years. Although some individuals are known to have lived past 200 years.

Fun fact

Leatherback turtles can dive to depths of 1000 metres, in search of food.Adult females return to land to lay their eggs after mating.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

Sea turtles may eat algae, sponges and sea squirts which all compete with coral polyps for space, light and nutrients, helping to promote a healthy coral reef.

Reef Sharks

There are 5 types of Reef shark: Blacktip, Whitetip,Grey, Caribbean and Silvertip. These sharks are classified as one of the most important species in the world by WWF, highlighting the importance of the part they play on coral reefs.

Food

Typically reef sharks hunt crustaceans (crabs, lobsters and shrimps), squid and most reef fishes.

Habitat

These sharks live on coral reefs in shallow tropical waters, up to depths of 80 metres.

Size

Up to 3 metres.

Life expectancy

12 - 25 years.

Fun fact

Reef sharks are viviparous, the young develop and grow inside the mother for around 1 year before the mother gives birth.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

They help maintain the balance of the food chain.

Lionfish

Lionfish or otherwise known as zebrafish, firefish or scorpion fish.

Food

Lionfish will eat anything that fits into their mouth. Their stomach can expand to 30 times its normal size.

Habitat

Lionfish are native to the South Pacific and Indian Ocean.They can survive in all marine habitats where temperatures exceed 10 degrees celsius.

Size

Up to 38 cm.

Life expectancy

Up to 15 years.

Fun fact

Female lionfish produce up to 2 million eggs per year. The egg sacs are covered in chemicals that prevent other fish from eating the eggs.Along with their ability to eat anything and high production rate, lionfish can be a huge threat to reefs theyâ€™re not native to.
Also, Lionfish can dive to depths of 305 metres.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

Lionfish are extremely well adapted predators. In areas which they are native to, they help control numbers of coral eating organisms such as parrotfish.

Parrotfish

Parrotfish are colourful fish that are part of the wrasse family. There are 80 identified species of parrotfish.

Food

Parrotfish have fused beak like teeth they use to break and grind down rock and dead coral. Parrotfish poop a fine white sand of undigested rock and coral skeleton.

Habitat

They can be found within both coral reef and rocky shores ecosystems, as well as seagrass beds, in tropical and subtropical waters.

Size

Up to 120cm.

Life expectancy

Up to 7 years.

Fun fact

Parrotfish can change sex multiple times through their lives.
Some species of parrotfish produce a mucus bubble, which they make using an organ in their head during the night. Scientists believe this mucus stops predators from smelling and finding the parrotfish.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

Although parrotfish do eat living coral, they prefer a diet of algae and dead coral. By grazing away at algae and dead coral parrotfish prevent algae from over growing and smothering live coral.

Clownfish and Sea Anenomes

Clown fish or otherwise known as anemone fish live with anemone, in warm tropical waters. Together they form a beneficial (symbiotic) relationship where the poisonous fish eating anemone provides the clown fish with protection from other fish whilst the crown fish cleans the anemone by eating dead tentacles and parasites.

Food

Clownfish are omnivores, they eat both plants and animals such as algae, small crustaceans (e.g. shrimps) and mollusks.
Anemone are carnivores, they lure in small fish or shrimp and use their stinging cells to paralysis and then eat their prey.

Habitat

Clownfish and anemones live in tropical waters. The Clownfish coats its skin a mucus that protects it from the anemones sting.

Size

Clownfish can grow up to 18 cm.

Life expectancy

Clownfish can live up to 10 years.

Fun fact

All clownfish are born male. When the dominant female dies, the largest male transforms into a female.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

They can feed on organisms such as algae that compete with coral polyps for food, space and nutrients.

Guard Crabs

Guard crabs are one of many species of guard crabs protecting their coral homes.They are found living on hard, branching coral across the indo-pacific area.

Food

Guard crabs eat mucus released by coral polyps as well as eat some of the polyps.

Habitat

On branched corals. They have a beneficial (symbiotic) relationship where the guard crab keeps the coral clean and protects the coral from predators such as Crown of Thorns Starfish whilst the branches of the coral provide shelter for the crab from predators.

Size

Up to 5 cm.

Life expectancy

Unknown.

Fun fact

Using their legs, guard crabs massage their hosts polyps until they release slimy mucus the crab eats.

Why are they important to coral reefs?

They protect the coral polyps from predators, maintaining the foundation of a coral reef ecosystem.
