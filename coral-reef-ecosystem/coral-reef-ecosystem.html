Coral reef

What is a coral reef?

These plant-looking structures are actually animals! A coral reef is made up of colonies of animals called coral polyps. They form when a coral polyp fixes itself to a rock or hard surface on the seafloor. The coral polyp then budsandmultiplies into a colony of identical clones--a single coral. Corals later join together to become a coral reef.

When coral polyps die and leave behind a hard limestone skeleton, a layer of the coral reef is built. Other coral polyps grow on top of this layer. Over a long period of time, coral reefs grow to become a diverse and colorful habitat to many marine animals and plants. Coral reefs make up only 0.2% of the world’s oceans, but 25% of all species in the ocean live in them!

Where can you find coral reefs?

Coral reefs thrive in clear, shallow, tropical areas, where they have access to a lot of sunlight. This is usually near land, and the optimal temperatures for them are between 68 - 82°F (20 - 28°C). The biggest coral reef in the world is the Great Barrier Reef off the coast to Australia.

What do coral polyps eat?

Coral polyps eat small plants and animals, such as algae, seagrass and plankton. They feed off algae especially. Algae and other plants need sunlight to photosynthesize. This is a process of converting sunlight into chemical energy. The chemical energy is then passed on to the corals through the food chain. Thus sunlight is an important source of energy for coral reefs.

Polyps also work together with phytoplankton, a type of microscopic algae, called zooxanthellae. The polyps are homes for the zooxanthellae and they also supply them with carbon dioxide, needed for the zooxanthellae to perform photosynthesis, through respiration. Respiration is a chemical process of obtaining energy for all living things to survive. In return, the zooxanthellae provide energy to the polyps through photosynthesis. This way of working together for biological mutual benefit is called symbiosis.

Types of coral reef

The earliest formed reef is the fringing reef which grows close to the coastline. It is the most commonly found reef.
The barrier reef forms later. It runs parallel to the coastline, but a body of water or a lagoon separates it from land.
The atoll reef is a ring-shaped reef or reef-island surrounding a lagoon. Often it forms around a volcanic island as a fringing reef. The atoll reef forms after the volcano becomes dormant and the volcanic island completely sinks into the sea.

Why are corals reefs important?

Coral reefs are important in that they:

  • They are home to a huge number of marine species.
  • Provide important nutrients and nitrogen for marine food chains
  • Produce half of the world's oxygen: an ingrediant for the air we breath
  • Protect coastlines from erosion, ocean waves and tropical storms
  • Source of materials to develop medicine and cures
